Weather Information Center

Understanding weather data and its impact on our daily lives

Temperature

Temperature is a measure of how hot or cold the air is. It's typically measured in Celsius (°C) or Fahrenheit (°F).

Key Facts:
  • The average global temperature is around 15°C (59°F)
  • The highest recorded temperature was 56.7°C (134°F) in Death Valley, USA
  • The lowest recorded temperature was -89.2°C (-128.6°F) in Antarctica
  • Temperature affects air pressure, humidity, and precipitation

Precipitation

Precipitation is any form of water that falls from clouds and reaches the ground, including rain, snow, sleet, and hail.

Key Facts:
  • Measured in millimeters (mm) or inches
  • The wettest place on Earth is Mawsynram, India, with about 11,871 mm (467.4 in) of rainfall annually
  • Precipitation forms when water vapor condenses in the atmosphere
  • Different types occur depending on temperature and atmospheric conditions

Humidity

Humidity is the amount of water vapor present in the air. Relative humidity is expressed as a percentage.

Key Facts:
  • 100% relative humidity means the air is saturated with water vapor
  • High humidity makes hot days feel hotter and cold days feel colder
  • Humidity affects our comfort, health, and even electronic devices
  • Dew point is the temperature at which air becomes saturated and condensation occurs

Wind

Wind is the horizontal movement of air from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure.

Key Facts:
  • Measured in kilometers per hour (km/h), miles per hour (mph), or knots
  • The Beaufort Scale classifies wind speeds from 0 (calm) to 12 (hurricane)
  • Wind direction is named for where it comes from, not where it's going
  • Local winds are influenced by terrain, bodies of water, and temperature differences

Atmospheric Pressure

Atmospheric pressure is the force exerted by the weight of air above a given point.

Key Facts:
  • Measured in hectopascals (hPa), millibars (mb), or inches of mercury (inHg)
  • Standard sea-level pressure is 1013.25 hPa (29.92 inHg)
  • High pressure typically brings clear, stable weather
  • Low pressure often brings clouds, precipitation, and storms

Cloud Cover

Cloud cover refers to the portion of the sky covered by clouds, usually measured in oktas or percentages.

Key Facts:
  • Clouds form when water vapor condenses around tiny particles in the air
  • The main cloud types are cumulus, stratus, cirrus, and nimbus
  • Clouds affect temperature by reflecting sunlight and trapping heat
  • Cloud patterns can help predict upcoming weather changes

Weather Forecasting

Weather forecasting is the application of science and technology to predict the state of the atmosphere for a given location.

Key Methods:
  • Numerical Weather Prediction: Uses mathematical models of the atmosphere and oceans
  • Statistical Methods: Based on historical weather patterns
  • Ensemble Forecasting: Runs multiple forecasts with slightly different initial conditions
  • Nowcasting: Very short-term forecasts (0-6 hours) using current observations

Modern forecasts combine satellite data, ground observations, radar, and supercomputers to provide increasingly accurate predictions.

Climate vs. Weather

While weather refers to short-term atmospheric conditions, climate describes the average weather patterns in a region over a long period.

Key Differences:
  • Time Scale: Weather is what you get today; climate is what you expect over decades
  • Variability: Weather can change rapidly; climate changes gradually
  • Prediction: Weather forecasts are specific; climate projections are statistical
  • Factors: Climate is influenced by global patterns like ocean currents and solar cycles

Understanding both weather and climate is essential for planning everything from daily activities to long-term infrastructure.
